Preparing Your Hives for the Spring Nectar Flow

Lucas Jensen March 15, 2026 7 min read

As the weather warms and the first pollen begins to arrive, your honeybee colonies are waking up from their winter cluster. Spring is arguably the most critical time for a beekeeper; the decisions you make now will directly impact your honey yield and the health of your hives for the rest of the year.

1. The First Inspection

Timing is everything. Do not open your hives until the temperature is consistently above 60°F (15°C) and the wind is calm. Breaking the propolis seal too early can chill the brood and set the colony back significantly.

During this first look, you want to accomplish three things rapidly:

  • Verify the presence of a laying queen (look for eggs, you don't necessarily have to find her).
  • Check food stores (do they need emergency fondant or syrup?).
  • Assess the cluster size to determine if the hive is viable.

2. Reversing Hive Bodies

Over the winter, bees naturally move upward as they consume their honey stores. By early spring, the cluster is often situated entirely in the top box, leaving the bottom box completely empty.

Reversing the boxes—placing the empty bottom box on top of the cluster—provides the illusion of space. This encourages the queen to move up and start laying, which helps alleviate the congestion that leads to early swarming.

3. Feeding Strategy

If your hives are light, you must feed them immediately. A 1:1 sugar syrup (one part sugar to one part water by weight) mimics a nectar flow and stimulates the queen to lay eggs rapidly.

"The goal in early spring is explosive population growth aligned perfectly with the start of the primary nectar flow. Missing this window by even two weeks can cost you an entire honey harvest." - Dr. Jonathan Smith

Stay vigilant, keep records, and don't hesitate to seek advice if something looks abnormal. Happy spring beekeeping!
